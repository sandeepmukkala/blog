My Journey Building AI Tools That Actually Matter

Most side projects die in a weekend. Mine turned into a mission. Here's the story of how personal frustration led me to build two AI-powered tools — and the lessons I learned along the way.

PB
Personal Blog Projects & Ideas

I've always been the kind of person who builds things to scratch my own itch. If something frustrates me enough, I'll eventually open a code editor and try to fix it. That instinct is how these projects were born — not as a grand plan, but as a series of personal projects that grew into something bigger than I expected.

This post is about that journey: the problems I kept running into, the tools I built to solve them, and the ideas I'm chasing next.

The Frustration That Started It All

Over seven years in enterprise engineering, I sat through thousands of meetings. Important decisions were made, action items were assigned, insights were shared — and then most of it vanished. Note-takers were too busy writing to participate. Zoom transcription didn't work for in-person meetings. And for multilingual teams, there was simply no tool that could transcribe a meeting in one language and summarize it in another.

I looked at what existed — and everything fell short in some way. Most tools required bots to join your calls (uncomfortable for many people). Others were English-only. Some were absurdly expensive. And none of them handled the simple case of "I'm sitting in an in-person meeting with my phone in my pocket."

So I did what any frustrated engineer would do: I built my own.

Project #1: Transcriptly

Transcriptly is a meeting transcription app I built from scratch — iOS, Android, and Web from a single codebase. The idea is dead simple: open the app, select a language, tap record, and put your phone down. It keeps recording even when your phone locks.

When the meeting ends, AI transcribes the audio, generates a structured summary with key points and action items, and — here's the part I'm most proud of — if the meeting was in a non-English language, it automatically provides both the original transcription AND an English translation. Dual output, zero extra steps.

It supports 14 languages today: English, Spanish, French, German, Portuguese, Japanese, Russian, Hindi, Tamil, Chinese, Arabic, Bengali, Kannada, and Telugu. You can also record offline and transcribe later when you have connectivity.

Building this taught me a lot about audio processing on mobile, cross-platform development, and the nuances of multilingual AI. It also taught me that the best product decisions come from being your own user.

The best tools don't ask you to change your workflow. They fit into the way you already work.

Project #2: Translatory

While building Transcriptly, I stumbled into an adjacent problem that was even more neglected: document translation.

Not the "paste text into a translation box" kind. I'm talking about real documents — contracts with watermarks, forms with handwritten fill-ins, invoices with company logos and borders, government documents with official stamps and signatures. When you translate these through existing tools, you get a wall of plain text. The layout is gone. The signatures are gone. The watermarks are gone. The document is fundamentally broken.

So I built Translatory — a format-preserving document translation tool. Upload an image, PDF, DOCX, or PPTX, and get back a fully translated document that looks like it was originally written in the target language. Watermarks, borders, logos, signatures — everything stays intact. 50+ languages supported.

This project pushed me deep into some of the hardest unsolved problems in document processing. It's the kind of unsexy engineering challenge that nobody talks about at conferences — but millions of people deal with every day.

What I Learned Building These Projects

A few things became clear to me after building both Transcriptly and Translatory:

  1. Start with the problem, not the technology. I didn't set out to "use AI." I set out to fix something that annoyed me. The technology was just the tool I reached for.
  2. Multilingual support isn't a feature — it's a responsibility. 75% of the world doesn't speak English. Building English-only tools means ignoring most of humanity.
  3. Privacy matters more than convenience. People are uncomfortable with bots in their meetings and their documents being sent to unknown servers. Privacy-first architecture isn't just ethical — it's a competitive advantage.
  4. Invest heavily in automated testing. When you're the only developer, a robust test suite is your safety net. It lets you move fast without breaking things.

What's Next: AI for Agriculture

Language barriers affect productivity. Food insecurity affects survival. The same AI capabilities I've built for language — computer vision, multilingual interfaces, mobile-first apps — are exactly what's needed on the farm.

With over 500 million smallholder farms worldwide feeding 80% of the global population, even small improvements in yield and efficiency can transform lives. I'm building AI tools that are accessible, affordable, and designed for the fields — not just the boardroom. This project is currently in progress. More details coming soon.

My Philosophy: Code with a Cause

"Code with a cause" is a personal rule I follow. Every project I take on has to pass a simple test: does this solve a real problem for real people?

I'm not interested in building the next chatbot wrapper or AI art generator. There are enough of those. I'm interested in the boring, hard, unglamorous problems — the ones that affect billions of people but don't make for viral demos.

Final Thoughts

We're at an incredible inflection point. AI costs have collapsed. Open-source models now rival proprietary ones. Cross-platform frameworks have matured to the point where a solo engineer can build for iOS, Android, and Web from a single codebase.

For the first time, one person with a laptop can build tools that genuinely compete with well-funded teams. The barriers have never been lower. The only question is: what will you build?

I choose to build tools that matter. Tools that help a consultant in Tokyo document her meeting in Japanese and share it in English. Tools that help a government clerk translate a legal document while preserving every signature and watermark. And this is just the beginning.

That's what this blog will be about — the projects I'm building, the ideas I'm exploring, the problems I'm trying to solve, and the lessons I learn along the way.

I'm just getting started.

If any of this resonates with you, I'd love to connect on LinkedIn. I'd love to hear what you're building too.

Thanks for reading.
